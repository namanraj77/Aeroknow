1.1 What Is a UAV, Really?

Plain English First

A UAV (Unmanned Aerial Vehicle) is simply an aircraft with no human pilot on board. Someone — or something — is still controlling it, either from the ground via radio link, or through onboard software that makes decisions autonomously. The key difference from a regular aircraft isn't that it flies differently; it's that removing the pilot changes everything about the design — you save weight, remove life-support needs, and can fly longer, higher, and into more dangerous places.

The broader term is UAS (Unmanned Aircraft System), which correctly captures that the flying vehicle is just one part of a larger system. You also need a ground station to send commands, a data link (radio) to carry those commands and send back video or sensor data, and a launch/recovery system. Fahlstrom & Gleason define UAS as "the integration of the air vehicle, payload, control element, and communications link into a functional system." Fahlstrom §2.1

Analogy

Think of a UAS like a television news crew. The camera operator (UAV) goes out to collect footage. The director in the production truck (ground control station) calls the shots via radio headset (data link). Without the truck and the headset, the camera operator is just wandering around. The UAV alone is not the system — the whole chain matters.

Why Does This Matter for Engineering?

Removing the pilot doesn't just mean removing a seat. It cascades through the entire design:

  • Weight reduction: No life support, ejection seat, pressurised cockpit, or oxygen system. This can save 200–500 kg on a large platform — weight you can spend on sensors, fuel, or weapons.
  • Endurance: Human pilots fatigue; autopilots do not. HALE UAVs like the RQ-4 Global Hawk fly for 32+ hours — impossible for a human crew without mid-air handoffs.
  • Risk tolerance: Losing a UAV is costly but not catastrophic in the way losing a pilot is. This enables missions into heavily defended airspace that would be unacceptable for manned aircraft.
  • Autonomy requirements: When the radio link breaks (jammed, blocked by terrain), the UAV must make its own decisions. This is the root motivation for all the navigation and AI content in later modules.

1.2 How UAVs Are Classified

Plain English First

There are UAVs ranging from a 33-gram pocket drone to a 15-tonne aircraft with a 40-metre wingspan. You cannot use the same rulebook for all of them. Military organisations use classification systems to group UAVs by capability — roughly: how big are they, how high do they fly, and how far do they go? These groupings then determine what rules apply, what missions they can do, and what it costs to lose one.

NATO Classification — STANAG 4670 / AEP-83

NATO uses a class system based primarily on MTOW (Maximum Take-Off Weight) and operating altitude. This is the standard used across all NATO member militaries for interoperability. Fahlstrom §2.2

Configuration Types — What Shape Is It?

Beyond weight, the physical shape of the aircraft determines what it can and cannot do. These are not interchangeable — each configuration is best suited to specific mission types: Sadraey §2.1

Fixed-Wing

Think of it like a regular airplane. Wings generate lift as the aircraft moves forward. Highly efficient — can stay airborne for 27+ hours on a tank of fuel. But it cannot hover; it must always be moving forward or it falls. Used for long-range ISR and strike.

Lift-to-Drag ratio (L/D): 15–30. At L/D = 20, only 5% of lift force goes to fighting air resistance. Fahlstrom §3.1

Multirotor

Think of it like a consumer DJI drone, scaled up. Rotors push air downward to hover. Can take off and land vertically, hover in place, and fly in any direction. Highly manoeuvrable at low speed. Very power-hungry — most military multirotors have 20–60 minute endurance at most.

Hover power: 150–250 W/kg. At that rate, batteries drain fast. Sadraey §2.3

Hybrid VTOL

Tries to get the best of both worlds. Takes off like a multirotor, then transitions to fixed-wing cruise. Think of a drone that tilts its rotors forward after takeoff. The trade-off: mechanically complex, and the transition phase is tricky to control automatically. Increasingly used for ship-based and logistics missions. Sadraey §2.4

Loitering Munition

A drone designed to crash into its target. It circles overhead (loiters) watching for a target, then dives into it carrying an explosive warhead. Unlike a missile, it can abort, circle longer, and re-engage. Unlike a traditional armed drone, it is disposable — built to be lost. Fahlstrom §2.5

Examples: Switchblade 300 (2.7 kg, 15 min), Switchblade 600 (22.7 kg, 40 min), HERO-120 (12.5 kg, 60 min).

1.3 The Weight Budget — How UAVs Are Sized

Plain English First

Every kilogram a UAV lifts off the ground has to "come from somewhere" — it's either payload, fuel, structure, electronics, or motors. The total weight has to equal the sum of all these parts. The engineering challenge is that all of these components are linked: add more payload, and you need a bigger motor, which needs more battery, which adds weight, which needs an even bigger motor. Engineers call this the "design spiral" — and it's how many UAV programs go over budget and over weight.

Sadraey formalises this dependency with the UAS weight equation: Sadraey §1.4

UAS Weight Budget Equation — Sadraey §1.4 $$W_0 = W_{PL} + W_E + f_s W_0 + f_p W_0 + f_{av} W_0$$

In plain English: Total weight ($W_0$) = payload weight + energy (fuel/battery) weight + structural weight + propulsion weight + avionics weight. The key insight is that structural, propulsion, and avionics weights are expressed as fractions of total weight ($f_s$, $f_p$, $f_{av}$) — meaning they grow automatically as the aircraft gets heavier.

Rearranging — collecting all $W_0$ terms on the left side:

$$\boxed{W_0 = \frac{W_{PL} + W_E}{1 - f_s - f_p - f_{av}}}$$

Critical insight: If the denominator $(1 - f_s - f_p - f_{av})$ approaches zero, $W_0$ approaches infinity. A design where structure + propulsion + avionics fractions sum to 1.0 is physically impossible — there is no room for payload or fuel. This is the mathematical form of the design spiral.

Analogy — The Backpack Problem

Imagine packing for a hike. Your backpack frame weighs 20% of whatever you load in it (it's a heavy-duty frame). Your water purifier weighs 10% of total load. Your food is 15% of total load. That leaves only 55% for actual useful gear (tent, clothes, sleeping bag). If the frame + purifier + food together exceeded 100% of pack capacity, you simply cannot pack — the backpack is infeasible. This is exactly what happens when $f_s + f_p + f_{av} \geq 1$.

Payload Mass Fraction — The Utility Metric

The Payload Mass Fraction (PMF) tells you what fraction of the aircraft's weight is actually useful. A higher PMF means a more efficient design — you're spending more of your weight budget on the mission. Fahlstrom §3.3

Platform ClassTypical PMFWhat This Means Practically

Here is why this matters concretely: a Group 1 mini-UAV at 9 kg MTOW with PMF = 0.10 carries just 0.9 kg of payload. A stabilised thermal imaging gimbal — the sensor you'd actually want for night ISR — weighs 1.2–2.5 kg. It literally cannot fit. This is not a problem that better software solves. It is a hard physics constraint.

Worked Example 1.1 — MQ-9B SkyGuardian Weight Budget

Let's verify the weight equation against published MQ-9B specs. We know the actual MTOW from GA-ASI's datasheet. Can the equation predict it?

ParameterValueSource / How Derived
MTOW $W_0$5,670 kgGA-ASI published specification
Max payload $W_{PL}$1,746 kgGA-ASI internal + external stores spec
Fuel mass $W_E$~1,800 kgEstimated from fuel capacity spec
Structural fraction $f_s$≈ 0.28Airframe + landing gear (estimated from similar aircraft)
Propulsion fraction $f_p$≈ 0.085TPE331-10 turboprop engine ~481 kg estimated
Avionics fraction $f_{av}$≈ 0.045FCC, comms, EW suite estimated

Step 1: Calculate the denominator: $1 - 0.28 - 0.085 - 0.045 = \mathbf{0.59}$

Step 2: Apply the equation: $W_0 = (1746 + 1800)/0.59 \approx \mathbf{6{,}010\text{ kg}}$

Result: Our prediction is 6,010 kg vs. the actual 5,670 kg — an error of about 6%. For conceptual design stage (where we're estimating fractions, not measuring real hardware), that's excellent accuracy. The 6% gap comes mostly from underestimating avionics weight (satellite communications hardware, electronic warfare suite, de-icing systems).

PMF: $1746/5670 = 0.308$ — about 31% of MQ-9B's weight is usable payload. That's why it can simultaneously carry a multi-sensor EO/IR turret, SAR radar pod, and air-to-ground missiles.

1.4 ISR Missions — Watching From Above

Plain English First

ISR stands for Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance. In plain terms: watching, listening, and gathering information about the enemy — from an aircraft that can stay up for 24+ hours without anyone getting tired. For ISR UAVs, the primary design goal is endurance (staying up as long as possible) and sensor coverage (seeing as much ground as possible). Speed matters much less.

Endurance — How Long Can It Stay Up?

For turboprop-powered fixed-wing UAVs like the MQ-9, endurance is calculated using the Breguet endurance equation. Before the math, here's the intuition: endurance improves when you (1) burn fuel slowly, (2) carry a lot of fuel, and (3) fly at the aerodynamically optimal speed — not too fast, not too slow. Fahlstrom §4.1

Breguet Endurance Equation — Fahlstrom §4.1 $$E = \frac{\eta_p}{c_p \cdot g} \cdot \left(\frac{C_L^{3/2}}{C_D}\right)_{\!\max} \cdot \sqrt{2\rho S} \cdot \left(\sqrt{W_i} - \sqrt{W_f}\right)$$

What each piece means:
$\eta_p$ = propeller efficiency — how much of engine power becomes thrust (0.82 for MQ-9).
$c_p$ = fuel burn rate per unit of power. Lower = more efficient engine.
$C_L^{3/2}/C_D$ = a measure of aerodynamic efficiency specifically for endurance (different from the efficiency that maximises range).
$W_i, W_f$ = weight at start and end of mission. The $\sqrt{}$ terms reflect diminishing returns as you burn fuel — the lighter aircraft becomes, the less fuel it needs to stay up.

Theorem 1.1 — A Common Pilot Mistake: Flying Too Fast for ISR

Here is a non-obvious result: the speed that maximises how long you stay up is NOT the same as the speed that maximises how far you go. For a propeller-driven UAV:

$$V_{\text{max endurance}}^* \approx 0.76 \times V_{\text{max range}}^*$$

Why? To maximise endurance, you want to minimise power consumption (power = drag × speed). To maximise range, you want to minimise fuel per kilometre (drag alone). These are different optimisation problems, solved at different speeds.

Mathematical derivation: Using the parabolic drag polar model $C_D = C_{D0} + K C_L^2$, the endurance-optimal lift coefficient is: $$C_L^{E*} = \sqrt{\frac{3C_{D0}}{K}} = \sqrt{3} \cdot C_L^{R*}$$ Since airspeed $V \propto 1/\sqrt{C_L}$, we get $V_E^* = V_R^* / 3^{1/4} \approx 0.76\, V_R^*$. Fahlstrom §4.2

Operational consequence: A ground controller who commands an ISR UAV at cruise speed instead of loiter-optimal speed is wasting roughly 4–5 hours of mission time on a 24-hour flight. That's the difference between catching an event and missing it.

Our equation works.

Sensitivity check: If engine fuel consumption ($c_p$) degrades by 5% (engines get less efficient as they age), endurance drops by ~5%: $27.2 \times 0.95 \approx 25.8$ hours — losing 1.4 hours of ISR coverage per mission. This is why engine maintenance directly impacts ISR capability.

Sensor Coverage — How Much Ground Does It See?

Staying airborne for 27 hours is only useful if the sensor is actually collecting intelligence. The size of the "patch" of ground the camera can see at any moment depends on how high you are, the camera's angle, and its field of view. Fahlstrom §5.1

Sensor Footprint and Coverage Rate — Fahlstrom §5.1 $$R_s = \frac{h}{\sin\delta} \qquad \text{(slant range — distance from camera to ground)}$$ $$W_{FP} = 2R_s\tan\!\tfrac{\theta_H}{2}, \quad L_{FP} = 2R_s\tan\!\tfrac{\theta_V}{2} \qquad \text{(footprint dimensions)}$$ $$\text{GSD} = \frac{p_{\mu m} \times R_s}{f_{mm} \times 10^3} \quad\text{[m/pixel]} \qquad \text{(how sharp is the image?)}$$ $$\text{PACR} = W_{FP}(1-f_{ov}) \times V_{GS} \times 3600 \quad\text{[m}^2\text{/hour]}$$

$h$: altitude above ground; $\delta$: sensor tilt angle from horizontal; $\theta_H, \theta_V$: horizontal/vertical camera field of view; $p_{\mu m}$: camera pixel size; $f_{mm}$: focal length; $f_{ov}$: image overlap (for stitching multiple images); $V_{GS}$: ground speed. GSD tells you the resolution — at GSD = 0.25 m/pixel you can identify a car type, but not read a number plate (need ~0.05 m/pixel for that).

1.5 Strike Missions and Swarm Doctrine

Plain English First

UAVs are not only used for watching — they are increasingly used for attacking. Two categories dominate current military thinking: loitering munitions (one drone, one precise strike) and swarms (hundreds of cheap drones acting together). Both represent fundamentally different approaches to the same problem: how do you destroy a target without risking a human life?

Loitering Munitions — The Drone Missile Hybrid

A loitering munition is best understood as a patient missile. A regular missile is fired and cannot be recalled. A loitering munition can patrol an area for 15–60 minutes, wait until the right target appears, and then dive into it. If the target disappears, it can abort. This patience is what makes them so operationally powerful — and legally complex. Fahlstrom §2.5

Guidance ModeHuman RoleLatency NeededLegal StatusExample
Man-in-the-loop Human presses fire button for every shot <500 ms link required Compliant with IHL Switchblade 300, 600
Man-on-the-loop System can auto-engage; human can abort 1–5 s tolerable Contested under IHL Harop (IAI)
Fully autonomous Human authorises the mission, not each shot No link needed Active legal debate Harpy NG (IAI)
Why Autonomy Raises Legal Questions International Humanitarian Law (IHL) requires that any attack on a target comply with three principles: Distinction (tell combatants from civilians), Proportionality (don't cause excessive civilian harm), and Precaution (take care before attacking). A human operator can make judgment calls on all three. A fully autonomous system must encode them in software — and whether current AI can reliably do that is deeply contested. As of 2024, no binding international law prohibits autonomous weapons, but 125+ countries have called for new rules. Fahlstrom §2.6

Swarm Systems — Power Through Numbers

A single sophisticated UAV can be shot down. But what if you sent 300 cheap ones simultaneously? Even if a defender destroys 100 of them, 200 still reach the target. This is the core idea behind drone swarm doctrine — saturation attack. Swarms are designed to be decentralised: no single command node controls all drones. Instead, each drone follows local rules that produce intelligent collective behaviour. Fahlstrom §2.7

Analogy — Murmuration of Starlings

Have you seen a flock of thousands of starlings moving as one fluid shape in the sky? No single bird is the leader. Each bird just follows three rules: don't crash into your neighbours, match their direction, and stay close to the group. The result looks like intelligent choreography, but it emerges entirely from simple local rules. Military drone swarms use the same principle — decentralised coordination through local rules, not a central commander. Fahlstrom §2.7

Reynolds' Flocking Rules — Mathematical Form

The formal version of the three flocking rules, defining forces on drone $i$ from its neighbours $\mathcal{N}$ within communication range $r$:

$$\mathbf{f}_{\text{sep}} = -\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_{r_s}} \frac{\mathbf{p}_j - \mathbf{p}_i}{\|\mathbf{p}_j - \mathbf{p}_i\|^2} \qquad \text{(push away from nearby drones)}$$ $$\mathbf{f}_{\text{align}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}|}\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}} \mathbf{v}_j - \mathbf{v}_i \qquad \text{(match their velocity)}$$ $$\mathbf{f}_{\text{cohesion}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}|}\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}} \mathbf{p}_j - \mathbf{p}_i \qquad \text{(move toward the group center)}$$ $$\mathbf{u}_i = w_s\mathbf{f}_{\text{sep}} + w_a\mathbf{f}_{\text{align}} + w_c\mathbf{f}_{\text{cohesion}} + w_m\mathbf{f}_{\text{mission}}$$

Military drones add a fourth term $\mathbf{f}_{\text{mission}}$ — an attraction toward the target and repulsion from threat zones (radar, missile batteries). The weights $w_s, w_a, w_c, w_m$ are tuned during testing. Get them wrong and the swarm either collides with itself ($w_s$ too small) or fragments and loses coordination ($w_c$ too small).

1.6 Threat Environments — What Tries to Stop Them

Plain English First

Military UAVs don't fly in safe airspace. They operate where adversaries actively try to destroy them, jam their communications, or feed them false position information. Understanding these threats is not optional — they define the entire design philosophy of military autonomous systems. This section covers the three most impactful threats: GPS jamming, GPS spoofing, and radar detection.

Threat 1 — GPS Jamming: Blinding the Navigation System

GPS signals arrive at Earth's surface incredibly weakly — about $-130$ dBm, which is roughly 10 billion times weaker than a WiFi signal. This makes GPS trivially easy to jam. A jammer just broadcasts loud radio noise on the same frequency, drowning out the satellite signals. The UAV's GPS receiver can't hear the satellites anymore and loses its position. Fahlstrom §3.5

GPS Jamming-to-Signal Ratio — Fahlstrom §3.5 $$\frac{J}{S} = \frac{P_j G_j}{P_s G_s} \cdot \left(\frac{R_s}{R_j}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{L_j}$$

What it means: $J/S$ is the ratio of jammer signal to GPS satellite signal at the UAV receiver. If $J/S \geq +25$ dB, the GPS is effectively denied. $P_j, G_j$: jammer power and antenna directivity. $P_s, G_s$: GPS satellite signal power and receive gain. $R_s \approx 20{,}200$ km: how far away GPS satellites are. $R_j$: distance to the jammer.

A basic 10 W jammer ($+40$ dBm) with an omnidirectional antenna at $R_j = 30$ km achieves $J/S \approx +55$ dB — far above the denial threshold. This means a $\$50$ handheld jammer bought online can deny GPS to a military UAV from 30 km away. This is why the entire Module 04 of this course exists.

Threat 2 — GPS Spoofing: Feeding False Positions

Jamming is obvious — the GPS receiver knows it's lost signal and raises an alarm. Spoofing is worse: the attacker broadcasts fake GPS signals that look real. The receiver thinks it knows exactly where it is — but the position is wrong. The UAV navigates confidently to the wrong location without any warning. This is what allegedly caused an Iranian capture of a US RQ-170 Sentinel in 2011 (though the specific method remains disputed). The defence requires cross-checking GPS against other sensors — barometers, terrain matching, inertial navigation — all topics covered in Module 04.

Threat 3 — Radar Detection: Being Seen

Ground-based radars illuminate UAVs with radio waves and detect the reflected signal. How well a UAV reflects radar is measured by its Radar Cross Section (RCS) in square metres. A conventional MALE UAV might have $\sigma \approx 1$ m² — detectable at ~200 km by a modern surveillance radar. Stealth shaping and radar-absorbent materials can reduce RCS to $0.01$ m², cutting detection range to ~63 km. Sadraey §2.6

Radar Range Equation — Detection Distance $$R_{\max} = \left[\frac{P_t G_t G_r \lambda^2 \sigma}{(4\pi)^3 P_{r,\min} L}\right]^{1/4}$$

Key insight from the 4th-root dependence: cutting RCS by 100× (20 dB) only reduces detection range by $100^{1/4} = 3.16\times$. You need enormous RCS reduction for modest range benefit — which is why stealth is difficult and expensive, and why it must be combined with other tactics (flying low, avoiding radar line-of-sight, limiting emissions).

The DDIL Environment — The Design Baseline The combination of GPS jamming, data link disruption, and active radar threats creates what military planners call a DDIL environment: Denied, Degraded, Intermittent, and Limited communications. Fahlstrom identifies DDIL as the primary reason military UAVs require onboard autonomy — the platform must complete its mission even when it cannot talk to anyone, cannot trust its GPS, and cannot be sure it hasn't been detected. Every module after this one is, at its root, a response to DDIL. Fahlstrom §3.6

    g_tx_dbi:        float = 30.0    # GCS antenna gain [dBi] → 1.2 m dish at C-band
    g_rx_dbi:        float = 6.0     # UAV antenna gain [dBi] → patch array
    misc_loss_db:    float = 3.0     # Cable and connector losses [dB]
    rx_sens_dbm:     float = -95.0   # Receiver sensitivity [dBm]
    req_margin_db:   float = 10.0    # Required link margin [dB]


class ISRAnalyser:
    """
    Full ISR mission analysis.
    Covers: weight budget, endurance, sensor geometry, GSD, PACR, link budget.
    """
    G       = 9.80665       # gravitational acceleration [m/s²]
    C_LIGHT = 299_792_458.0 # speed of light [m/s]
    R_EARTH = 6_371_000.0   # mean Earth radius [m]

    def __init__(self, platform: Platform, sensor: EO_Sensor, link: DataLink):
        self.p = platform
        self.s = sensor
        self.l = link
        # Compute air density at cruise altitude using ISA troposphere model
        self.rho = self._isa_density(platform.cruise_alt_m)

    def _isa_density(self, h_m: float) -> float:
        """
        International Standard Atmosphere density at altitude h [m].
        Valid in troposphere (h < 11,000 m). Lapse rate = 6.5 K/km.
        """
        T = 288.15 - 0.0065 * h_m          # temperature at altitude [K]
        # density ratio from barometric formula
        rho = 1.225 * (T / 288.15) ** 4.256
        return rho

    def weight_budget(self) -> dict:
        """
        Sadraey §1.4 — closed-form MTOW from component fractions.
        Predicts gross take-off weight and validates against actual spec.
        """
        p = self.p
        denominator = 1 - p.f_struct - p.f_prop - p.f_avionics
        W0_predicted = (p.w_payload_kg + p.w_fuel_kg) / denominator
        error_pct = abs(W0_predicted - p.mtow_kg) / p.mtow_kg * 100
        PMF = p.w_payload_kg / p.mtow_kg
        return {
            'denominator':   denominator,
            'W0_predicted':  W0_predicted,
            'W0_actual':     p.mtow_kg,
            'error_pct':     error_pct,
            'PMF':           PMF,
        }

    def endurance_hours(self) -> float:
        """
        Breguet endurance — Fahlstrom §4.1, Eq. 4.7.
        Maximum endurance condition: (CL^1.5 / CD)_max.
        Note: this differs from max-range condition (CL/CD)_max.
        """
        p = self.p
        W_i = p.mtow_kg              # initial weight (full fuel + full payload)
        W_f = p.mtow_kg - p.w_fuel_kg  # final weight (fuel burned, payload stays)

        E_seconds = (
            (p.eta_prop / (p.bsfc * self.G))  # propulsion efficiency term
            * p.cl32_cd_max                    # aerodynamic efficiency term
            * np.sqrt(2 * self.rho * p.wing_area_m2)  # altitude term
            * (np.sqrt(W_i) - np.sqrt(W_f))   # fuel weight change term
        )
        return E_seconds / 3600.0

    def sensor_geometry(self) -> dict:
        """
        Fahlstrom §5.1 — Sensor footprint, slant range, GSD, coverage rate.
        Assumes flat Earth (acceptable for typical UAV sensor geometry).
        """
        s = self.s
        h = self.p.cruise_alt_m

        dep_rad  = np.radians(s.depression_deg)
        hfov_rad = np.radians(s.hfov_deg)
        vfov_rad = np.radians(s.vfov_deg)

        R_slant = h / np.sin(dep_rad)                     # slant range [m]
        W_fp    = 2 * R_slant * np.tan(hfov_rad / 2)     # footprint width [m]
        L_fp    = 2 * R_slant * np.tan(vfov_rad / 2)     # footprint length [m]

        # Ground Sample Distance — image resolution at this range
        GSD_m = (s.pixel_pitch_um * 1e-6 * R_slant) / (s.focal_length_mm * 1e-3)

        # Persistent Area Coverage Rate (accounting for image overlap)
        PACR_km2ph = (W_fp * (1 - s.overlap_frac) * self.p.ground_speed_ms * 3600) / 1e6

        return {
            'R_slant_km':   R_slant / 1000,
            'W_fp_m':       W_fp,
            'L_fp_m':       L_fp,
            'GSD_cm':       GSD_m * 100,
            'PACR_km2ph':   PACR_km2ph,
        }

    def link_budget(self) -> dict:
        """
        Fahlstrom §5.3 — Friis transmission equation for LOS link budget.
        Computes maximum RF range and compares to geometric horizon.
        """
        lk = self.l

        # Total available link budget [dB]
        allowed_path_loss = (lk.p_tx_dbm + lk.g_tx_dbi + lk.g_rx_dbi
                             - lk.misc_loss_db - lk.rx_sens_dbm - lk.req_margin_db)

        # Convert path loss budget to maximum range using Friis equation
        wavelength = self.C_LIGHT / (lk.freq_ghz * 1e9)
        R_rf_m = (wavelength / (4 * np.pi)) * 10 ** (allowed_path_loss / 20)

        # Geometric LOS horizon at cruise altitude
        R_horizon_m = np.sqrt(2 * self.R_EARTH * self.p.cruise_alt_m)

        limiting = 'horizon-limited' if R_horizon_m < R_rf_m else 'power-limited'

        return {
            'allowed_path_loss_dB': allowed_path_loss,
            'R_rf_km':             R_rf_m / 1000,
            'R_horizon_km':        R_horizon_m / 1000,
            'limiting_factor':     limiting,
        }

    def report(self):
        """Print a formatted analysis report."""
        p  = self.p
        wb = self.weight_budget()
        E  = self.endurance_hours()
        sg = self.sensor_geometry()
        lb = self.link_budget()

        print(f"\n{'='*56}")
        print(f"  ISR MISSION ANALYSIS — {p.name}")
        print(f"{'='*56}")
        print(f"\n  Altitude: {p.cruise_alt_m/0.3048:.0f} ft  |  Air density: {self.rho:.4f} kg/m³")

        print(f"\n  ── WEIGHT BUDGET (Sadraey §1.4) ──────────────────")
        print(f"  Denominator:    {wb['denominator']:.3f}  (must be > 0)")
        print(f"  Predicted W0:   {wb['W0_predicted']:.0f} kg")
        print(f"  Actual W0:      {wb['W0_actual']:.0f} kg  (error: {wb['error_pct']:.1f}%)")
        print(f"  Payload frac:   {wb['PMF']:.3f}  ({wb['PMF']*100:.1f}% of MTOW is useful payload)")

        print(f"\n  ── ENDURANCE (Fahlstrom §4.1) ─────────────────────")
        print(f"  Breguet E:      {E:.2f} hours on station")

        print(f"\n  ── SENSOR GEOMETRY (Fahlstrom §5.1) ───────────────")
        print(f"  Slant range:    {sg['R_slant_km']:.2f} km")
        print(f"  Footprint:      {sg['W_fp_m']:.0f} m × {sg['L_fp_m']:.0f} m")
        print(f"  GSD:            {sg['GSD_cm']:.1f} cm/pixel  (recognition needs < 45 cm)")
        print(f"  PACR:           {sg['PACR_km2ph']:.0f} km²/hour")
        print(f"  Mission total:  {sg['PACR_km2ph']*E:.0f} km² over {E:.1f} hr mission")

        print(f"\n  ── DATA LINK (Fahlstrom §5.3) ─────────────────────")
        print(f"  Path loss bdgt: {lb['allowed_path_loss_dB']:.0f} dB")
        print(f"  Max RF range:   {lb['R_rf_km']:.0f} km")
        print(f"  Geom. horizon:  {lb['R_horizon_km']:.0f} km")
        print(f"  Conclusion:     {lb['limiting_factor']}")
        print(f"\n  (Both ranges ≈ equal → extending range needs relay or SATCOM)")
        print(f"{'='*56}\n")
