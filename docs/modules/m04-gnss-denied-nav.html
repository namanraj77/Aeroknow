4.1 The Problem — Why GPS Cannot Be the Only Answer

Plain English First

GPS works by receiving radio signals from satellites 20,000 km above Earth. By the time those signals reach the ground, they are incredibly faint — about 10 billion times weaker than a typical WiFi signal. Any radio transmitter powerful enough to operate in the same frequency band can drown out those signals entirely. This is GPS jamming, and it costs almost nothing. A more sophisticated attack — GPS spoofing — sends fake GPS signals that trick the receiver into reporting a completely wrong position with full confidence. In a modern contested battlefield, both attacks happen routinely. A UAV that trusts GPS unconditionally in this environment will fly somewhere it should not, or crash.

The Asymmetry That Makes This a Hard Problem

The fundamental asymmetry of GPS jamming is captured by the J/S ratio from Module 01. Let's quantify exactly how easy denial is in practice: Fahlstrom §3.5

GPS Signal Budget at the Receiver $$P_{GPS} \approx -130 \text{ dBm} \approx 10^{-16} \text{ W} \qquad \text{(GPS L1 at Earth's surface)}$$ $$\frac{J}{S}\bigg|_{dB} = (P_j + G_j) - (P_{GPS} + G_{rx}) + 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{R_s}{R_j}\right)$$

A 10 W handheld jammer at 30 km range achieves $J/S \approx +55$ dB — far above the +25 dB denial threshold. This means GPS can be denied by non-state actors using equipment costing under $100. Military GPS receivers use controlled reception pattern antennas (CRPAs) and military encryption (M-code) which raise the denial threshold to ~+40 dB — but a 100 W directed jammer still wins at any range under ~80 km. 4.2 IMU Dead Reckoning — And Why It Always Drifts

Plain English First

When GPS is lost, the first fallback is the IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit) — a chip that measures acceleration and rotation rates. By integrating (accumulating) those measurements over time, you can track how position has changed from a known starting point. This is called dead reckoning, and sailors have used it for centuries. The problem: integration amplifies errors. A tiny constant bias in the accelerometer — say, 0.01 m/s² — becomes a velocity error of 0.6 m/s after one minute, and a position error of 18 metres after one minute. After 10 minutes: 1,800 metres of error. Dead reckoning alone is not a solution; it is a bridge between better measurement updates.

Analogy — Walking Blindfolded

Imagine walking blindfolded and trying to track your position by counting footsteps and estimating which direction each step goes. After 10 steps you're reasonably accurate. After 100 steps your small angular errors have compounded into a big directional error. After 500 steps you have no idea where you are. An IMU does exactly this — but at 1000 measurements per second. The integration errors still compound just as inexorably; they just accumulate faster.

IMU Error Sources — The Allan Variance Model

IMU errors are characterised using Allan variance analysis, which separates the noise into distinct physical processes: Bestaoui Sebbane §7.2

IMU Stochastic Error Model — Allan Variance $$\sigma^2(\tau) = \frac{N^2}{\tau} + B^2 \frac{\tau}{3} + \frac{K^2\tau}{3}$$

Three noise regimes:
$N$: Angle/Velocity Random Walk — white noise from electronics. Averages down with $\sqrt{\tau}$. Units: deg/√hr (gyro) or m/s/√hr (accel).
$B$: Bias Instability — low-frequency 1/f noise. Irreducible minimum error floor. Units: deg/hr or m/s².
$K$: Rate Random Walk — slowly wandering bias. Grows with $\sqrt{\tau}$. Units: deg/hr/√hr or m/s²/√hr.

Position Error Growth — Why Drift Is Unavoidable

The key result that every navigation engineer must internalise: position error from accelerometer bias grows quadratically with time. Bestaoui Sebbane §7.3

Dead Reckoning Position Error from Accelerometer Bias $$\delta v(t) = b_a \cdot t \qquad \text{(velocity error grows linearly)}$$ $$\delta p(t) = \frac{1}{2} b_a \cdot t^2 \qquad \text{(position error grows quadratically)}$$

$b_a$: accelerometer bias [m/s²]. Even after gravity subtraction and frame rotation corrections, residual bias remains. Attitude error $\delta\theta$ also contributes to position error via gravity vector misalignment: $\delta p^{gravity}(t) \approx \frac{1}{2}g\,\delta\theta^2\,t^2$. For a MEMS-grade IMU ($b_a \approx 0.05$ m/s²), position error after 60 seconds = 90 m. For a navigation-grade IMU ($b_a \approx 5\times10^{-5}$ m/s²), error after 60 seconds = 0.09 m — but these cost $10,000–$100,000 and weigh 500 g–2 kg. The tactical trade-off is stark.

4.3 Bayesian State Estimation — The Conceptual Foundation

Plain English First

The IMU drifts. The camera is noisy. The barometer fluctuates with weather. No single sensor gives you a perfect answer. The right approach is to combine all of them, weighting each by how much you trust it. If the IMU has been running for 10 seconds and has accumulated large drift, trust it less. If the camera just matched a clear landmark, trust that more. Bayesian estimation is the formal mathematical framework for doing exactly this — maintaining a probability distribution over "where am I?" and updating it as new evidence arrives.

Analogy — A Doctor Diagnosing a Patient

A doctor doesn't diagnose from a single test. They start with a prior belief based on symptoms, update it when blood tests come back, update it again when the scan result arrives, and at each stage the diagnosis reflects all evidence weighted by reliability. Bayesian estimation does exactly the same thing for UAV position — it maintains a running "best guess" probability distribution that gets updated every time a new sensor measurement arrives, weighted by how reliable that sensor is.

Formally, Bayesian estimation maintains the posterior distribution $p(\mathbf{x}_k \mid \mathbf{z}_{1:k})$ — the probability of being in state $\mathbf{x}_k$ given all measurements up to time $k$: Bestaoui Sebbane §7.4

Recursive Bayesian Update $$\underbrace{p(\mathbf{x}_k \mid \mathbf{z}_{1:k})}_{\text{posterior}} \propto \underbrace{p(\mathbf{z}_k \mid \mathbf{x}_k)}_{\text{likelihood}} \cdot \int \underbrace{p(\mathbf{x}_k \mid \mathbf{x}_{k-1})}_{\text{motion model}} \cdot \underbrace{p(\mathbf{x}_{k-1} \mid \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1})}_{\text{previous posterior}} \, d\mathbf{x}_{k-1}$$

Reading this in plain English: "My new belief about where I am equals my measurement's likelihood times my prediction from the motion model times my previous belief, summed over all possible previous states."

Why it's hard: The integral is analytically tractable only for linear-Gaussian systems (Kalman filter). For nonlinear systems (real UAVs), we approximate. Three main approximations: EKF (linearise), UKF (sigma points), Particle filter (Monte Carlo). Each trades off computational cost vs. accuracy in nonlinear regimes. MDPI Autonomous Control Ch.3

4.4 Extended Kalman Filter — Step by Step

Plain English First

The Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) is the most widely used navigation algorithm in the world — it runs inside every smartphone, GPS receiver, and military navigation system. It works in two alternating phases: Predict (use the IMU to guess where you'll be next) and Update (use a measurement to correct that guess). The "extended" part means it handles nonlinear systems by taking a first-order Taylor series approximation — linearising the equations around the current best estimate. This works well when the estimate is reasonably accurate, and breaks down only in highly nonlinear regimes or with very poor initialisation.

The State Vector

First, we define what the EKF is tracking. For full navigation: Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5

Navigation EKF State Vector (16 states) $$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{p}^T & \mathbf{v}^T & \mathbf{q}^T & \mathbf{b}_a^T & \mathbf{b}_g^T \end{bmatrix}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$$

$\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^3$: position (NED frame)  ·  $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$: velocity (NED frame)  ·  $\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^4$: attitude quaternion (unit constraint → 3 DOF effective)  ·  $\mathbf{b}_a \in \mathbb{R}^3$: accelerometer bias  ·  $\mathbf{b}_g \in \mathbb{R}^3$: gyroscope bias.

The bias states are crucial — by estimating and correcting for sensor bias in real time, the EKF continuously calibrates the IMU during flight. This is called "in-flight calibration" and is why a well-tuned EKF produces much better navigation than raw IMU integration, even without external corrections.

The Two-Step EKF Algorithm

▸ Step 1: Predict (IMU Propagation, ~1000 Hz)

Use the IMU reading to propagate the state forward one time step. This is cheap — just integrate the kinematic equations from Module 02. The uncertainty (covariance $P$) grows because IMU noise is added each step.

▸ Step 2: Update (Measurement Correction, ~10–100 Hz)

When a new measurement arrives (camera feature match, barometer reading, etc.), compute the Kalman gain and correct both the state estimate and covariance. The covariance shrinks — new information reduces uncertainty.

EKF Predict Step — Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5 $$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k-1} = f\!\left(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1|k-1},\, \mathbf{u}_k\right)$$ $$P_{k|k-1} = F_k\, P_{k-1|k-1}\, F_k^T + Q_k$$

$f(\cdot)$: nonlinear state transition function (IMU kinematic equations — integrate position, velocity, quaternion). $F_k = \partial f/\partial \mathbf{x}\big|_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}}$: Jacobian of $f$ — the linearisation that makes EKF "extended." $Q_k$: process noise covariance matrix — populated from IMU noise specs (Allan variance parameters). $P_{k|k-1}$: predicted covariance — uncertainty grows during prediction.

EKF Update Step — Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5 $$\mathbf{y}_k = \mathbf{z}_k - h\!\left(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k-1}\right) \qquad \text{(innovation)}$$ $$S_k = H_k\, P_{k|k-1}\, H_k^T + R_k \qquad \text{(innovation covariance)}$$ $$K_k = P_{k|k-1}\, H_k^T\, S_k^{-1} \qquad \text{(Kalman gain)}$$ $$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k} = \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k-1} + K_k\, \mathbf{y}_k$$ $$P_{k|k} = (I - K_k H_k)\, P_{k|k-1}$$

$\mathbf{z}_k$: actual measurement; $h(\cdot)$: expected measurement given state (measurement model); $\mathbf{y}_k$: innovation — "how surprised were we by the measurement?"; $H_k = \partial h/\partial \mathbf{x}$: measurement Jacobian; $R_k$: measurement noise covariance (from sensor specs); $K_k$: Kalman gain — the optimal weighting between prediction and measurement.

Theorem 4.1 — Kalman Gain as Optimal Weighting

The Kalman gain $K_k$ minimises the mean squared estimation error. It can be interpreted as a certainty ratio:

$$K_k = \frac{P_{k|k-1} H_k^T}{H_k P_{k|k-1} H_k^T + R_k} = \frac{\text{predicted uncertainty}}{\text{predicted uncertainty} + \text{measurement noise}}$$

Two limiting cases:
If $R_k \to 0$ (perfect sensor): $K_k \to H_k^{-1}$ — trust the measurement completely, ignore the prediction.
If $P_{k|k-1} \to 0$ (perfect model): $K_k \to 0$ — trust the prediction completely, ignore the measurement.
In practice, both have noise, and $K_k$ blends them by their relative uncertainties. This is the optimal linear estimator — no filter can do better for linear-Gaussian systems. Bestaoui Sebbane §7.6

Worked Example 4.2 — Kalman Gain for Altitude Fusion

At time step $k$, the EKF predicts altitude 52.3 m with uncertainty $P_{zz} = 4.0$ m². A barometer reads 51.1 m with measurement noise $R = 2.25$ m² (1.5 m std dev). Compute the Kalman gain, the updated altitude, and the updated uncertainty.

For scalar altitude, $H = 1$ (measurement directly observes altitude). So:

Innovation covariance: $S = H P_{zz} H^T + R = 1 \times 4.0 \times 1 + 2.25 = \mathbf{6.25}$ m²

Kalman gain: $K = P_{zz} H^T / S = 4.0 / 6.25 = \mathbf{0.64}$ (64% weight to the measurement)

Innovation: $y = 51.1 - 52.3 = -1.2$ m

Updated altitude: $\hat{z} = 52.3 + 0.64 \times (-1.2) = 52.3 - 0.768 = \mathbf{51.53}$ m

Updated uncertainty: $P_{zz}^+ = (1 - 0.64) \times 4.0 = 0.36 \times 4.0 = \mathbf{1.44}$ m² (std dev drops from 2.0 m to 1.2 m)

Interpretation: Neither the prediction (52.3 m) nor the measurement (51.1 m) is fully trusted. The fused estimate (51.53 m) sits between them, closer to the measurement because it was weighted 64%. The uncertainty shrank from 4.0 to 1.44 m² — the measurement gave us new information.

4.5 Unscented Kalman Filter — When EKF Linearisation Fails

Plain English First

The EKF approximates nonlinear functions by a straight line (first-order Taylor series). This is fine when the function is gently curved near the current estimate. But for highly nonlinear systems — like attitude estimation during aggressive manoeuvres, or bearing-only navigation — the linearisation is too crude and the filter diverges. The UKF (Unscented Kalman Filter) solves this by not linearising at all. Instead, it carefully selects a small set of sample points — called sigma points — passes each one through the exact nonlinear function, then reconstructs the mean and covariance from the results. It's like sampling a few representative points to understand a curve, rather than drawing a tangent line at one point.

Sigma Points — The Key Idea

For a state of dimension $n$ with mean $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ and covariance $P$, the UKF generates $2n + 1$ sigma points: MDPI Autonomous Control Ch.3

UKF Sigma Point Selection $$\mathcal{X}^{(0)} = \hat{\mathbf{x}}$$ $$\mathcal{X}^{(i)} = \hat{\mathbf{x}} + \left(\sqrt{(n+\lambda)P}\right)_i, \quad i = 1,\ldots,n$$ $$\mathcal{X}^{(i+n)} = \hat{\mathbf{x}} - \left(\sqrt{(n+\lambda)P}\right)_i, \quad i = 1,\ldots,n$$

$\lambda = \alpha^2(n + \kappa) - n$: scaling parameter. $\left(\sqrt{(n+\lambda)P}\right)_i$: $i$-th column of the matrix square root (Cholesky factor). Each sigma point is a deterministic sample that captures the mean and covariance exactly. The points are propagated through the nonlinear function $f(\cdot)$ exactly — no Jacobian required.

Theorem 4.2 — UKF vs EKF Accuracy

For a scalar nonlinear function $y = f(x)$ with Gaussian input $x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[f(x)] = f(\mu) + \frac{1}{2}f''(\mu)\sigma^2 + O(\sigma^4) \qquad \text{(true mean)}$$ $$\mathbb{E}_{EKF}[f(x)] = f(\mu) \qquad \text{(EKF approximation — first order)}$$ $$\mathbb{E}_{UKF}[f(x)] = f(\mu) + \frac{1}{2}f''(\mu)\sigma^2 + O(\sigma^4) \qquad \text{(UKF — captures second order)}$$

The UKF captures the second-order term $\frac{1}{2}f''(\mu)\sigma^2$ that the EKF misses. This term becomes significant when the uncertainty $\sigma^2$ is large (e.g., poor GPS initialisation or after a long GNSS-denied period). In those conditions, EKF under-estimates the mean error and diverges; UKF remains accurate. Computational cost: UKF requires $2n+1$ function evaluations per step (vs 1 for EKF) plus a matrix square root — roughly 2–4× more expensive than EKF for typical navigation state dimensions. 4.6 Visual-Inertial Odometry (VIO)

Plain English First

Visual-Inertial Odometry combines a camera with an IMU to estimate how the UAV is moving — without any external reference like GPS. The camera sees the world and tracks how visual features move between frames. The IMU provides rapid, high-rate motion measurements between camera frames. Together, they lock each other's weaknesses: the IMU drifts over time but is fast; the camera doesn't drift but is slow and can blur. The result is a navigation solution that can maintain sub-metre accuracy for several minutes in a fully GPS-denied environment — which is transformative for military UAVs operating in jammed or urban environments.

The VIO Cost Function — Tightly Coupled Formulation

Tight coupling means camera features and IMU measurements are fused in a single nonlinear least-squares optimisation, rather than two separate filters: Bestaoui Sebbane §7.8

VIO Joint Cost Function (MSCKF / Sliding Window Smoother) $$\min_{\mathbf{x}_{0:T},\, \mathbf{p}_{1:N}} \underbrace{\sum_{k=1}^{T} \left\| \mathbf{r}_{IMU}^{(k)} \right\|^2_{Q_k^{-1}}}_{\text{IMU preintegration residuals}} + \underbrace{\sum_{k=0}^{T} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{V}_k} \left\| \mathbf{z}_{k,i} - \pi\!\left(R_k^T(\mathbf{p}_i^W - \mathbf{t}_k)\right) \right\|^2_{\Sigma_z^{-1}}}_{\text{Visual reprojection residuals}}$$

First term — IMU residuals: $\mathbf{r}_{IMU}^{(k)}$: difference between predicted and measured IMU preintegration. Penalises inconsistency between IMU readings and the estimated trajectory.

Second term — Visual reprojection: $\mathbf{z}_{k,i}$: observed pixel location of feature $i$ in frame $k$. $\pi(\cdot)$: camera projection function (projects 3D world point to 2D pixel). $\mathbf{p}_i^W$: 3D position of feature $i$ in world frame. $(R_k, \mathbf{t}_k)$: camera pose at time $k$. The residual is "where did I expect to see this feature vs. where I actually saw it?" This nonlinear least-squares problem is solved iteratively (Gauss-Newton or LM algorithm).

4.7 Terrain-Aided Navigation — Matching the Ground Below

Plain English First

Terrain-Aided Navigation (TAN) is an elegant idea: every patch of terrain on Earth is unique — hills, valleys, and flatlands form a fingerprint. If a UAV measures the terrain elevation profile below it (using a radar altimeter or LiDAR) and compares it to a stored digital map, it can figure out exactly where it is — with no GPS, no radio transmission, and no landmarks needed. This is essentially the navigation equivalent of reading a map by feel, in complete radio silence. The Tomahawk cruise missile has used terrain matching since the 1980s; modern military UAVs combine it with VIO for continuous drift-free navigation.

TERCOM — Terrain Contour Matching

TERCOM was developed for the Tomahawk BGM-109 cruise missile and is the foundational terrain navigation algorithm. It works by correlating a measured terrain profile against stored DTED (Digital Terrain Elevation Data): Bestaoui Sebbane §7.9

TERCOM Mean Absolute Difference Correlation $$\text{MAD}(x, y) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left| h_m(i) - h_s(x + i\Delta x,\; y) \right|$$ $$\hat{x}_{pos}, \hat{y}_{pos} = \arg\min_{x,y} \text{MAD}(x, y)$$

$h_m(i)$: measured terrain elevation profile (from radar altimeter or LiDAR) at $N$ sample points; $h_s(x,y)$: stored DTED elevation at grid coordinate $(x,y)$; $\Delta x$: along-track spacing between samples. The position fix is the map location that best matches the measured profile. Accuracy is limited by DTED resolution: 30 m (DTED-2), 10 m (DTED-3), 1 m (high-resolution). Works best in rough terrain; fails in flat plains (no distinguishing features).

SITAN — Continuous EKF-Based Terrain Navigation

SITAN (Sandia Inertial Terrain-Aided Navigation) extends TERCOM by integrating terrain matching into an EKF, providing continuous position corrections rather than discrete fixes: Bestaoui Sebbane §7.10

SITAN Measurement Model $$z_k = h_{altimeter,k} - h_s(x_k, y_k) + v_k, \qquad v_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_k)$$ $$H_k = \left[\frac{\partial h_s}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial h_s}{\partial y},\; 0,\; \ldots\right] = \nabla h_s(x_k, y_k)$$

The EKF measurement is the difference between measured clearance (altitude above terrain) and the stored terrain height at the estimated position. The measurement Jacobian $H_k$ is the terrain surface gradient — the steeper the terrain, the more position information each altimeter reading provides. This is why TAN accuracy improves dramatically over rough terrain and degrades over plains. The terrain gradient is computed by finite differencing the DTED grid.

4.9 Python: EKF Navigation with VIO Updates

What This Code Does Implements a 2D EKF for GNSS-denied navigation (simplification for clarity — the full 3D version follows the same structure with 16 states). State: position (x, y), velocity (vx, vy), heading $\psi$, accelerometer biases $(b_x, b_y)$. IMU runs at 100 Hz; VIO corrections arrive at 10 Hz. Compares dead-reckoning vs EKF+VIO error over a 60-second figure-8 trajectory. Run with: pip install numpy matplotlib
Python 
# ekf_gnss_denied.py
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# 2D EKF navigation in GNSS-denied environment.
# Implements: Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5–7.6
# State: [x, y, vx, vy, psi, b_ax, b_ay]  (7 states)
# IMU propagation: 100 Hz
# VIO position update: 10 Hz
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

import numpy as np

class EKFNavigator:
    """
    Extended Kalman Filter for 2D GNSS-denied navigation.
    Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5

    State vector x = [x, y, vx, vy, psi, b_ax, b_ay]:
      x, y    : position [m] in world frame
      vx, vy  : velocity [m/s] in world frame
      psi     : heading [rad]
      b_ax,b_ay: accelerometer bias [m/s²]
    """

    def __init__(self, dt: float = 0.01):
        self.dt = dt
        self.n  = 7          # state dimension

        # Initial state estimate (start at origin, stationary, level)
        self.x = np.zeros(self.n)

        # Initial covariance — moderate uncertainty on position/velocity
        self.P = np.diag([1.0, 1.0,     # position uncertainty (m²)
                          0.1, 0.1,     # velocity uncertainty (m²/s²)
                          0.01,          # heading uncertainty (rad²)
                          1e-4, 1e-4])  # bias uncertainty (m²/s⁴)

        # Process noise Q — set from IMU Allan variance specs
        # Position noise: dominated by velocity uncertainty integration
        # Velocity noise: from accelerometer noise floor (0.05 m/s² RMS)
        # Heading noise: from gyro noise floor (0.001 rad/s RMS)
        # Bias noise: random walk (1e-5 m/s² per root-second)
        self.Q = np.diag([1e-4, 1e-4,    # position process noise
                          2.5e-3, 2.5e-3,# velocity process noise
                          1e-6,           # heading process noise
                          1e-8, 1e-8])  # bias random walk noise

        # VIO measurement noise — position accuracy ~3 cm (0.03 m)
        self.R_vio = np.diag([0.03**2, 0.03**2])

    def predict(self, accel_body: np.ndarray, yaw_rate: float):
        """
        IMU propagation step — Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5 Eq. 7.12
        accel_body: [ax, ay] measured by IMU in body frame [m/s²]
        yaw_rate:   measured by gyro [rad/s]
        """
        x, y, vx, vy, psi, b_ax, b_ay = self.x
        dt = self.dt

        # Subtract estimated bias from IMU reading
        ax_corr = accel_body[0] - b_ax
        ay_corr = accel_body[1] - b_ay

        # Rotate body-frame acceleration to world frame
        cos_psi, sin_psi = np.cos(psi), np.sin(psi)
        ax_w = ax_corr * cos_psi - ay_corr * sin_psi
        ay_w = ax_corr * sin_psi + ay_corr * cos_psi

        # State propagation (Euler integration)
        self.x = np.array([
            x  + vx*dt + 0.5*ax_w*dt**2,
            y  + vy*dt + 0.5*ay_w*dt**2,
            vx + ax_w*dt,
            vy + ay_w*dt,
            psi + yaw_rate*dt,
            b_ax,    # bias modelled as random walk (constant + noise)
            b_ay,
        ])

        # Jacobian F = ∂f/∂x — linearisation of the motion model
        # Most entries are the identity or simple derivatives
        F = np.eye(self.n)
        # Position depends on velocity
        F[0, 2] = dt;   F[1, 3] = dt
        # Position and velocity depend on heading (rotation of body accel)
        da_dpsi_x = (-ax_corr * sin_psi - ay_corr * cos_psi)
        da_dpsi_y = ( ax_corr * cos_psi - ay_corr * sin_psi)
        F[0, 4] = 0.5 * da_dpsi_x * dt**2
        F[1, 4] = 0.5 * da_dpsi_y * dt**2
        F[2, 4] = da_dpsi_x * dt
        F[3, 4] = da_dpsi_y * dt
        # Velocity depends on bias (negative, since we subtract bias)
        F[2, 5] = -cos_psi * dt;   F[2, 6] =  sin_psi * dt
        F[3, 5] = -sin_psi * dt;   F[3, 6] = -cos_psi * dt

        # Covariance propagation: P = F P F^T + Q
        self.P = F @ self.P @ F.T + self.Q

    def update_vio(self, pos_meas: np.ndarray) -> float:
        """
        VIO position measurement update — Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5 Eq. 7.16
        pos_meas: [x, y] position from VIO [m]
        Returns: innovation magnitude (diagnostic)
        """
        # Measurement model: h(x) = [x, y] — directly observe first two states
        H = np.zeros((2, self.n))
        H[0, 0] = 1.0   # observe x position
        H[1, 1] = 1.0   # observe y position

        # Innovation: z - h(x_predicted)
        innovation = pos_meas - self.x[:2]

        # Innovation covariance: S = H P H^T + R
        S = H @ self.P @ H.T + self.R_vio

        # Kalman gain: K = P H^T S^{-1}  (Theorem 4.1)
        K = self.P @ H.T @ np.linalg.inv(S)

        # State update
        self.x = self.x + K @ innovation

        # Covariance update: P = (I - KH) P (Joseph form for numerical stability)
        IKH = np.eye(self.n) - K @ H
        self.P = IKH @ self.P @ IKH.T + K @ self.R_vio @ K.T

        return float(np.linalg.norm(innovation))


def simulate_gnss_denied(T: float = 60.0, dt: float = 0.01):
    """
    Simulate 60-second GNSS-denied navigation on a figure-8 trajectory.
    Compares three navigation solutions:
    1. IMU-only dead reckoning (no filter, no corrections)
    2. EKF with VIO updates at 10 Hz
    3. Ground truth (perfect knowledge)
    """
    N   = int(T / dt)
    t   = np.linspace(0, T, N)
    ekf = EKFNavigator(dt)

    # Storage arrays
    true_pos  = np.zeros((N, 2))
    imu_pos   = np.zeros((N, 2))
    ekf_pos   = np.zeros((N, 2))

    # IMU-only dead reckoning state (no filter)
    imu_vel  = np.zeros(2)
    imu_p    = np.zeros(2)
    imu_psi  = 0.0

    # Persistent IMU bias (simulates MEMS accelerometer bias)
    TRUE_BIAS = np.array([0.008, 0.005])   # [m/s²] — tactical MEMS grade

    for k in range(N):
        tk = k * dt

        # ── Ground truth: figure-8 Lissajous trajectory ───────────
        omega = 2 * np.pi / T
        true_pos[k] = np.array([
            20 * np.sin(2 * omega * tk),
            10 * np.sin(4 * omega * tk),
        ])

        # True accelerations (analytical derivative of trajectory)
        true_accel_w = np.array([
            -(2*omega)**2 * 20 * np.sin(2*omega*tk),
            -(4*omega)**2 * 10 * np.sin(4*omega*tk),
        ])
        true_yaw_rate = 0.05 * np.sin(2*omega*tk)

        # ── Simulate noisy IMU measurement ────────────────────────
        # IMU measures body-frame acceleration with bias + white noise
        psi_true = 0.1 * np.sin(2*omega*tk)   # true heading
        c, s = np.cos(psi_true), np.sin(psi_true)
        R_w2b = np.array([[c, s], [-s, c]])     # world → body rotation
        true_accel_body = R_w2b @ true_accel_w

        imu_accel = true_accel_body + TRUE_BIAS + np.random.randn(2) * 0.05
        imu_gyro  = true_yaw_rate   + np.random.randn() * 0.001

        # ── IMU-only dead reckoning (no corrections) ─────────────
        ax_w = imu_accel[0]*np.cos(imu_psi) - imu_accel[1]*np.sin(imu_psi)
        ay_w = imu_accel[0]*np.sin(imu_psi) + imu_accel[1]*np.cos(imu_psi)
        imu_vel += np.array([ax_w, ay_w]) * dt
        imu_p   += imu_vel * dt
        imu_psi += imu_gyro * dt
        imu_pos[k] = imu_p

        # ── EKF: predict step (IMU) ───────────────────────────────
        ekf.predict(imu_accel, imu_gyro)

        # ── EKF: VIO update at 10 Hz (every 10th IMU step) ───────
        if k % 10 == 0 and k > 0:
            # VIO gives position with small noise (~3 cm)
            vio_meas = true_pos[k] + np.random.randn(2) * 0.03
            ekf.update_vio(vio_meas)

        ekf_pos[k] = ekf.x[:2]

    # ── Compute error statistics ────────────────────────────────
    imu_errors = np.linalg.norm(imu_pos - true_pos, axis=1)
    ekf_errors = np.linalg.norm(ekf_pos - true_pos, axis=1)

    print(f"\n{'='*56}")
    print(f"  GNSS-DENIED NAVIGATION — ERROR ANALYSIS")
    print(f"  Bestaoui Sebbane §7.5–7.6  |  dt={dt}s  T={T}s")
    print(f"{'='*56}")
    print(f"\n  IMU bias simulated: [{TRUE_BIAS[0]:.3f}, {TRUE_BIAS[1]:.3f}] m/s² (tactical MEMS)")
    print(f"\n  {'Metric':<30} {'IMU-Only':>10} {'EKF+VIO':>10}")
    print(f"  {'-'*50}")
    print(f"  {'Final error':<30} {imu_errors[-1]:>9.2f}m {ekf_errors[-1]:>9.3f}m")
    print(f"  {'Mean error':<30} {np.mean(imu_errors):>9.2f}m {np.mean(ekf_errors):>9.3f}m")
    print(f"  {'Max error':<30} {np.max(imu_errors):>9.2f}m {np.max(ekf_errors):>9.3f}m")
    print(f"  {'RMS error':<30} {np.sqrt(np.mean(imu_errors**2)):>9.2f}m {np.sqrt(np.mean(ekf_errors**2)):>9.3f}m")
    reduction = np.mean(imu_errors) / (np.mean(ekf_errors) + 1e-9)
    print(f"\n  Error reduction from EKF+VIO: {reduction:.0f}×")
    print(f"  Final bias estimate: [{ekf.x[5]:.4f}, {ekf.x[6]:.4f}] m/s²")
    print(f"  True bias:          [{TRUE_BIAS[0]:.4f}, {TRUE_BIAS[1]:.4f}] m/s²")
    print(f"  Bias estimation accuracy: {abs(ekf.x[5]-TRUE_BIAS[0])/TRUE_BIAS[0]*100:.1f}% error")
    print(f"\n  → EKF estimates IMU bias in real time (in-flight calibration).")
    print(f"{'='*56}\n")
    return t, true_pos, imu_pos, ekf_pos


if __name__ == "__main__":
    np.random.seed(42)
    simulate_gnss_denied(T=60.0, dt=0.01)
