What Is a Drone?

Everyone has seen one. Few people really know what it is. In this module, we start from zero — no physics, no engineering background required. By the end, you'll understand what a drone actually is, how it stays in the air, and why militaries around the world have made it their most important new tool.

1.1 Cadet Arjun's First Encounter

📖 Mission Log — Day 1

Arjun was seventeen when he saw it for the first time. Not in a movie. Not on a screen. In the actual sky above the Aero India airshow in Bengaluru, a matte-grey aircraft banked silently overhead — no cockpit, no pilot, no engine noise anyone could place. It moved too precisely to be random. Too smoothly to be a toy.

"What is that?" he asked the officer standing beside him.

The officer didn't look up. "That," she said, "is the most important aircraft your generation will ever learn to operate."

That was the day Arjun decided he needed to understand drones. Not just fly them — understand them. This module is where that education begins.

1.2 What Is a Drone, Exactly?

The word "drone" is informal slang. The correct technical term is UAV — Unmanned Aerial Vehicle. Some documents call it a UAS (Unmanned Aircraft System), which includes not just the aircraft but everything that makes it work: the ground control station, the radio link, and the software.

Think of it this way

A UAV is to a normal aircraft what a remote-controlled car is to a regular car — except the UAV can also drive itself, using onboard computers instead of a human behind a controller. Some UAVs need a pilot on the ground at all times. Others can fly an entire mission with nobody touching the controls.

There is one critical thing that makes a UAV different from every aircraft before it: the pilot is not at risk. The aircraft can be shot down, captured, or crashed — and the person who flew it walks away unharmed. That single fact has transformed how militaries think about air power.

📖 Mission Log — The Officer Explains

"Before UAVs," the officer told Arjun, "if you wanted a camera over hostile territory, you sent a pilot into the danger zone. Shoot down the plane, you lose the pilot. That limits everything — the missions you'll attempt, the risks you'll accept, the decisions you'll make."

"Now you send the machine. The pilot sits in an air-conditioned room, 5,000 kilometres away. The machine gets shot down?" She shrugged. "You build another one."

1.3 Why Doesn't It Fall Out of the Sky?

Every aircraft — from a paper plane to a fighter jet to a military drone — is a battle between four forces. Understanding these four forces is the foundation of all aerospace engineering.

Analogy — The Tug of War

Imagine four people pulling on a drone in four different directions. If the forces balance, the drone hovers perfectly still. If one side pulls harder, the drone moves that way. A pilot's entire job — human or computer — is to manage these four forces.

▸ THE FOUR FORCES — Interactive Diagram

What Each Force Does

Lift is generated by the rotors. Each rotor spins fast enough to push air downward. Air pushed down = drone pushed up. This is Newton's Third Law in action: every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Weight is simply gravity — Earth pulling the drone downward. The heavier the drone (and its payload), the more lift the rotors must generate just to hover.

Thrust is what moves the drone forward. A quadcopter achieves this by tilting slightly — some of the rotor's lift force is now directed forward instead of straight up.

Drag is air resistance. The faster a drone moves, the more the air pushes back against it. Aerodynamic shape design is all about minimising drag.

Why Hover = Balance

When a drone hovers perfectly still, Lift exactly equals Weight and Thrust exactly equals Drag. The instant the pilot (or autopilot) wants to move in any direction, they tip this balance — deliberately. All of drone flight is controlled imbalance.

1.4 The Family of Military Drones

Not all military UAVs look like the quadcopter in your neighbour's garden. Military drones span an enormous range — from a device small enough to fit in a soldier's backpack to aircraft heavier than a bus.

📖 Mission Log — The Hangar Tour

The officer walked Arjun through a hangar. Along one wall sat machines of every size. A palm-sized device that looked like a toy. A sleek fixed-wing aircraft the length of a car. Something that resembled a miniature helicopter. And at the far end, almost touching the ceiling, a vehicle with a wingspan wider than a bus.

"Same category," she said. "All UAVs. Different jobs."

Group 1 — Nano/Micro

Pocket Drones

Under 20 kg. Fits in a backpack. Used by infantry for over-the-hill reconnaissance — see what's around the corner before you walk into it.

Example: Black Hornet Nano
Group 2 — Small

Tactical UAVs

20–55 kg. Launched by a small team. Provides real-time video to ground commanders during active operations.

Example: RQ-11 Raven
Group 3 — Medium

Theatre UAVs

55–600 kg. Operates at altitude for hours. Covers large areas. Feeds intelligence to commanders many levels up the chain.

Example: MQ-1C Grey Eagle
Group 4-5 — Large/MALE

Strategic UAVs

600 kg+. Can carry weapons and sensors. Flies for 24+ hours. Replaces entire squadrons of manned aircraft for persistent surveillance.

Example: MQ-9 Reaper
Loitering Munition

Kamikaze Drones

A drone that is the weapon. Flies to a target area, circles waiting for the right moment, then dives and explodes. One-way mission.

Example: Harop, Shahed-136
Swarm Systems

The Swarm

Dozens or hundreds of small drones working together as one system. Overwhelm air defences. Coordinate attacks. No single point of failure.
