When GPS Fails

GPS is the nervous system of modern drone navigation. And in any serious military conflict, your enemy will try to destroy it. In this module you will learn exactly how GPS works, how jamming and spoofing attack it, and how military engineers build drones that can navigate accurately even when every satellite signal has been deliberately erased.

4.1 The Blank Screen

📖 Mission Log — Day 19

The exercise was supposed to be routine. Arjun was in the operations room watching three drone feeds simultaneously when all three map displays went blank at once. The position indicators — three small icons showing where each drone was — simply vanished.

The operators didn't panic. They had trained for this.

“Jammer active,” said one, already switching to the backup navigation display. “Visual confirmed — all three are still flying. Switching to INS mode.”

Arjun leaned over. The drones were still moving on the screen — but now their positions were being calculated by something other than GPS. They were navigating using their own sensors alone, like submarines running blind underwater.

“How long can they hold position without GPS?” Arjun asked.

“Depends on the platform,” said the operator. “Best systems, maybe twenty minutes before drift becomes a problem. Worst systems, two minutes.” She paused. “That's why GPS-denied navigation is the hardest engineering problem in military UAV design right now.”

4.2 How GPS Actually Works — And Why It's Fragile

To understand why GPS fails, you first need to understand how it works. Most people know that GPS uses satellites — but the details of how reveal exactly why it is so easy to attack.

The Lighthouse Analogy

Imagine you are lost at sea in complete darkness. Three lighthouses on the coast are all flashing signals. You can measure the time it takes each signal to reach you. Since light travels at a known speed, you can calculate your exact distance from each lighthouse. Where three circles of equal distance overlap — that is your position.

GPS works identically, but in three dimensions, with satellites instead of lighthouses, and with radio signals instead of light flashes.

► HOW GPS CALCULATES POSITION — Trilateration Minimum 4 satellites required for 3D fix

Here is the vulnerability buried in the diagram: the GPS signal arriving at your drone is extraordinarily weak. By the time a satellite signal travels 20,000 km from orbit to Earth, it has spread out so much that its power at the receiver is roughly 10–16 watts — that is 0.0000000000000001 watts. A single watt of radio interference can overwhelm it completely.

⚠ The Fundamental Weakness of GPS

GPS was designed in the 1970s for peacetime navigation, not warfare. The signal strength was never engineered to resist deliberate interference. Today, a cheap consumer radio transmitter — costing a few hundred dollars — can deny GPS to everything within several kilometres. This is the defining vulnerability of modern drone warfare.

4.3 Jamming — Blinding the Drone

GPS jamming is the simplest and most common form of attack. A jammer broadcasts radio noise on the same frequencies that GPS satellites use. The drone's receiver cannot distinguish the satellite signal from the noise — it simply loses its position fix and shows no GPS.

📖 Mission Log — Understanding the Attack

“Think of it like shouting in a library,” the instructor explained to Arjun's group. “The GPS satellite is whispering from 20,000 kilometres away. The jammer stands next to the drone and screams on the same frequency. The drone can't hear the whisper anymore. That's all jamming is — noise on the right frequency.”

“So the drone knows it's being jammed?” asked Arjun.

“It knows it has no GPS. It doesn't know why. Could be buildings, could be terrain, could be a jammer. That uncertainty is the problem.”

Jamming: Key Facts

Effect on drone: GPS position fix lost. Drone falls back to INS/barometer/optical flow navigation.
Range: A 1-watt portable jammer — the size of a TV remote — can deny GPS within ~5 km. Military-grade jammers cover hundreds of km.
Detectability: The drone can detect that GPS is missing but usually cannot instantly locate the jammer. However, the jammer is actively transmitting — which makes it detectable by direction-finding equipment. Turning on a jammer reveals your position.
Real-world use: Russia fields truck-mounted systems such as Krasukha-4 capable of disrupting GPS across entire operational areas. IISS, 2023

4.4 Spoofing — The More Dangerous Threat

GPS spoofing is more sophisticated and far more dangerous than jamming. Instead of drowning out the GPS signal with noise, a spoofer transmits fake GPS signals that mimic real satellites. The drone receives what appears to be a valid position fix — but the position is wrong.

The Forged Map Analogy

Jamming is like tearing the map out of the drone's hands — it knows it is lost. Spoofing is like replacing the map with a forged one. The drone confidently navigates using the fake map, heading exactly where the attacker wants it to go, with no idea anything is wrong.

This is why spoofing is considered the more serious threat: a jammed drone enters failsafe mode and returns home. A spoofed drone could be redirected to fly over a populated area, land in enemy territory, or crash into a physical obstacle.

⚠ Spoofing: The Invisible Attack

A spoofed drone shows a normal GPS fix. All systems show green. The autopilot is confident. The operator may not realise anything is wrong until the drone arrives at the wrong location — or does not arrive at all. Detection requires cross-checking GPS against independent navigation sources (IMU dead reckoning, visual landmark matching, or a second independent receiver) — which is exactly why military drones have multiple redundant navigation systems.

4.5 How Military Drones Navigate Without GPS

Every serious military UAV programme has invested heavily in GPS-denied navigation — the ability to navigate accurately with no satellite signal whatsoever. There are four main approaches, each with different strengths, limitations, and engineering costs.

◆ INS / Dead Reckoning

Inertial Navigation

Uses the IMU's accelerometers and gyroscopes to calculate position from a known starting point — just as sailors once did by tracking speed and heading over time.

✅ Works everywhere — no external signals needed
❌ Drift accumulates over time (~1–3 m/min for MEMS)
📷 Visual Odometry

Camera-Based Navigation

Downward or forward cameras track feature points in the environment frame-by-frame to estimate motion — like how your eye tracks movement when you are moving.

✅ Low drift, no external signals
❌ Fails in darkness, featureless terrain, dust
🌍 Terrain Matching

Map Correlation

Compares real-time sensor data (radar altimeter, camera, lidar) with a pre-loaded digital terrain map. Used in cruise missiles for decades.

✅ Very accurate over known terrain
❌ Requires pre-mission mapping; fails in urban canyons
🔌 Alternative Signals

Non-GPS Radio Nav

Uses eLoran, cellular towers, TV broadcast signals, or dedicated military positioning systems as GPS alternatives when satellites are unavailable.

✅ Works when GPS is denied
❌ Infrastructure-dependent; not universally available
The Military Answer: Sensor Fusion

No single GPS-denied method is reliable enough on its own. Modern military UAVs use sensor fusion — combining IMU dead reckoning, visual odometry, terrain matching, barometer, and magnetometer data through a mathematical filter (usually an Extended Kalman Filter). Each source covers the weaknesses of the others. The result is a position estimate that degrades gracefully rather than failing catastrophically when GPS is lost. This fusion architecture is covered in full in Module B05.
