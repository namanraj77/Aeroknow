3.2 What Is a Flight Controller?

A flight controller (FC) is a small computer — often no larger than a credit card — mounted at the centre of the drone. It is the single most important component on the aircraft. Remove the engines and the drone is grounded. Remove the flight controller and the drone is not just grounded — it is uncontrollable.

Think of It Like Your Inner Ear

Close your eyes and stand on one foot. You don't fall — your inner ear senses your balance and your muscles make hundreds of tiny corrections every second without you consciously commanding them. That is exactly what a flight controller does.

It reads sensors 400 to 8,000 times per second — measuring tilt, acceleration, rotation, altitude, and position — then sends tiny adjustments to each motor to keep the drone level, stable, and on course. All of this happens without any human input.

Every time the wind pushes the drone sideways, every time a rotor speed varies by a fraction, every time the drone drifts from its intended path — the flight controller notices within a millisecond and corrects. The human operator only sets the destination. The flight controller handles the physics, constantly, silently, at machine speed.

3.3 The Six Core Sensors

The flight controller is only as good as the data it receives. Six sensors form the essential sensing suite of any serious UAV — each measuring something different, each contributing a piece of the drone's self-awareness.

📖 Mission Log — Opening the Drone

The technician cracked open the drone's body panel and pointed to components on the flight controller board. “That one's the IMU,” she said, tapping a chip smaller than a fingernail. “It knows if you're tilting, spinning, or accelerating. Faster than you can blink.” She closed the panel. “Six sensors. Together they give the autopilot a complete picture of where it is and how it's moving — twenty times per second, minimum.”

◈ IMU

Inertial Measurement Unit

Contains a gyroscope (rotation rate) and accelerometer (linear acceleration). Runs at up to 8,000 Hz. The backbone of all stabilisation.

⚡ Works with zero external signals — fastest sensor on the drone
▲ Barometer

Barometric Altimeter

Measures air pressure, which drops with altitude. Accurate to ~30 cm in calm conditions. Fully GPS-independent.

⚡ Holds altitude in GPS-denied zones
✦ GPS / GNSS

Satellite Navigation

Receives signals from GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, or BeiDou satellites to compute latitude, longitude, altitude. Accurate to ~1–3 m in open sky.

⚠ Vulnerable to jamming and spoofing — see B04
⊕ Magnetometer

Electronic Compass

Detects Earth's magnetic field to determine heading direction. Without it, the drone cannot reliably hold a course.

⚠ Disrupted by metal structures and RF interference
⊞ Optical Flow

Ground-Tracking Camera

A downward-facing camera measuring ground movement to determine horizontal drift. Works like an optical mouse — GPS-free at low altitude.

⚡ GPS-free position hold under 50 m altitude
»» Lidar / Sonar

Range Finder

Emits laser (Lidar) or sound (sonar) pulses and times the echo return to measure exact distance to a surface below or ahead.

⚡ Precision landing, terrain following, obstacle avoidance
⚠ Military Vulnerability — GPS is Not Guaranteed

In a contested environment, enemy forces can jam or spoof GPS signals within hundreds of kilometres. A drone that depends entirely on GPS becomes useless the moment a jammer activates. This is why military drones carry multiple redundant sensors. The IMU, barometer, optical flow, and magnetometer can all maintain basic flight with GPS completely denied. 3.4 PID Control — How the Brain Makes Decisions

Knowing where you are is one thing. Knowing how to get back to where you want to be — smoothly, quickly, without overshooting — is another. The algorithm that solves this is called PID control. It runs inside every flight controller ever made.

The Shower Analogy

You step into a shower and the water is too cold. You turn the hot tap. Too little — still cold. Too much — it scalds. You overshoot, undershoot, then settle. A PID controller does this automatically, faster, and for six axes simultaneously: it considers how far off you are, how fast you're approaching the target, and whether you've been drifting for a long time.

P Proportional

Reacts to how far from target. 10° off level → apply a correction proportional to 10°. Big error = big correction.

Drone: “I am 10° off. Apply force.”
I Integral

Reacts to how long error has persisted. Drifting left for 2 seconds → add extra correction to cancel accumulated drift.

Drone: “I've been off a while. Add more.”
D Derivative

Reacts to how fast error is changing. Correcting rapidly toward level → ease off before overshooting. The braking term.

Drone: “I'm correcting fast — slow down.”
PID in Numbers

A quadcopter runs separate PID loops for roll, pitch, yaw, altitude, and position — at 400 Hz or higher. That is 400 sense-think-act cycles per second, per axis. Simultaneously. Beard & McLain, 2012

Open Loop vs Closed Loop

Open-loop: send a command, assume it worked, no checking. Closed-loop: continuously measure the outcome and correct. Every serious autonomous system — drone, missile, self-driving car — is closed-loop. Without feedback, any disturbance compounds into failure.
