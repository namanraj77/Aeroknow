Drones That Decide

A drone that can fly, navigate, and see is still just a tool — unless it can make decisions on its own. In this module you will learn the spectrum of autonomy from remotely piloted to fully autonomous, how the Sense-Plan-Act loop drives all autonomous behaviour, how waypoint navigation works, and why keeping a human in the loop remains the central challenge of military drone ethics and doctrine.

8.1 The Comm Dropout

📖 Mission Log — Day 48

The drone was 70 kilometres out when the communication link went silent. Not a GPS dropout — the flight controller handled that automatically. This was the command link: the channel through which the operator told the drone what to do next.

Arjun watched the operator's hands. They didn't move to the controls. She was watching the mission display calmly.

"Shouldn't you take over?" he asked.

"Take over with what?" she said. "I have no link. Nothing I type goes anywhere. The drone is on its own."

"So what happens?"

"It decides." She pointed at the display. The drone icon had already changed course — smoothly, deliberately, turning back toward the home base. "We pre-programmed the contingency. If link is lost for more than 45 seconds: complete current waypoint, then return to home and hold at 500 metres. That's what it's doing. Nobody told it to do that just now. It chose, based on its programming."

Arjun stared at the screen. A machine, 70 kilometres away, making decisions. "How autonomous is it, exactly?"

"That," she said, "is the most important question in this entire field."

What You'll Know After This Module

You will understand the spectrum of autonomy from remotely piloted to fully autonomous, how the Sense-Plan-Act loop drives all autonomous drone behaviour, how waypoint navigation works under the hood, what the human-in-the-loop debate involves, and why these questions sit at the intersection of engineering, law, and ethics.

8.2 The Spectrum of Autonomy

Autonomy is not binary — it is a spectrum. A drone can be remotely piloted for everything, autonomous for nothing, or anywhere in between. Military systems are deliberately designed to operate at a specific point on this spectrum depending on the mission, the legal framework, and the tactical situation.

Full Human Control Human Supervised Full Autonomy
Remotely PilotedHuman controls every input in real time
Semi-AutonomousHuman sets goals, drone executes
Human-on-the-LoopDrone acts, human can override
Fully AutonomousDrone senses, decides, acts alone
L1 Remotely Piloted

Human Flies Everything

Every control input comes from a human operator in real time. The drone is an extension of the pilot's hands. No communication link = drone is uncontrolled.

Example: DJI consumer drone in manual mode, early military RPVs
L2 Semi-Autonomous

Human Sets Goals

The operator defines waypoints and missions. The drone executes the flight autonomously, but the human decides the objectives. Loss of link triggers failsafe.

Example: MQ-9 Reaper on a pre-planned ISR route
L3 Human-on-the-Loop

AI Acts, Human Supervises

The AI makes and executes decisions automatically. A human monitors and can override, but is not required to approve each individual action. Speed is the advantage.

Example: Autonomous obstacle avoidance, terrain following systems
L4 Fully Autonomous

Machine Decides Alone

No human in the loop at all. The drone senses its environment, makes all decisions, and executes them without any human approval or oversight during the mission.

Example: Loitering munitions in terminal phase, some swarm experiments
⚠ The Legal and Ethical Line

International Humanitarian Law (IHL) requires that lethal force be applied with human judgment — a machine cannot be held accountable for a decision to use force. This is why most nations maintain a formal policy requiring a human to authorise any lethal action, even when the drone is otherwise fully autonomous. The debate over Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS) is one of the most consequential policy debates in modern defence. ICRC, 2021

8.3 The Sense-Plan-Act Loop

Every autonomous system — regardless of its complexity — operates on the same fundamental three-step cycle: Sense-Plan-Act (SPA). This loop runs continuously, hundreds of times per second for low-level flight control, or once per few seconds for high-level mission decisions.

The Driver Analogy

Sense: You glance at the road — another car is braking ahead. Plan: You decide to brake. Act: Your foot presses the pedal. Then the loop repeats: you glance again, process the new situation, decide, act. Driving is an endless Sense-Plan-Act loop. So is autonomous drone flight — except the cycle runs at machine speed and never tires.

► THE SENSE-PLAN-ACT LOOP — Multi-Layer Architecture Different time scales for different decisions

The key insight: autonomy is not one loop — it is a hierarchy of nested loops, each operating at a different time scale. The control loop (PID) runs in under 1 millisecond. The guidance loop runs every 10-100 milliseconds. The mission loop runs every few seconds. Each layer feeds commands to the layer below and receives feedback from the environment. This architecture is consistent across every modern autonomous system, from consumer drones to military UAVs.

8.4 Waypoint Navigation — How Autonomous Routes Work

Waypoints are the fundamental unit of autonomous mission planning. A mission is simply an ordered list of waypoints, each with associated actions: hover here for 30 seconds, take a photo, descend to 100 metres, loiter for 10 minutes, return home. The drone executes the list sequentially without operator input.

The Turn-by-Turn Navigation Analogy

Entering a destination into your phone's navigation app is waypoint planning. The app breaks the journey into a sequence of steps — turn left in 300 metres, merge onto the motorway, take exit 14 — that you follow one at a time. You do not re-plan the whole route every second; you just follow the next step. Drone waypoint navigation works identically, except the "steps" are GPS coordinates and the "driver" is the autopilot.

Conditional Waypoints — Decision Points in the Mission

Modern mission planners support conditional logic: "If battery drops below 30%, skip to waypoint 8 (RTH). If target detected at waypoint 4, loiter for 20 minutes and alert operator. If link lost, execute failsafe route." These conditional branches turn a simple waypoint list into a genuine decision tree — the drone chooses its path based on the state of the mission. This is the bridge between scripted autonomy and genuine decision-making.

8.5 Human in the Loop — The Central Debate

As drones become more capable of acting autonomously, the question of where the human sits in the decision chain becomes increasingly critical — especially when those decisions involve the use of force.

📖 Mission Log — The Ethics Briefing

"Three positions on the spectrum," the ethics officer told the group. "Human-in-the-loop: the machine identifies the target, the human authorises the strike. Every engagement, human approval required. Human-on-the-loop: the machine identifies and engages automatically, but the human can abort within a defined window. Human-out-of-the-loop: the machine does everything, the human is informed after the fact."

"Which one do we use?" someone asked.

"Depends on the system, the country, the legal framework, and the speed of the threat," she said. "A missile incoming at Mach 3 does not give you 30 seconds to get a human to press a button. But a vehicle driving down a road at 60 km/h probably does. The engineering question and the ethical question are inseparable."

⚠ Speed vs Accountability — The Core Tension

The faster a threat moves, the less time exists for human decision-making. A hypersonic missile gives seconds. An incoming drone swarm gives minutes. A vehicle convoy gives hours. Military doctrine must calibrate the required level of human oversight to the time available — while maintaining legal and ethical accountability. There is no universally correct answer. This is an active, unresolved debate in every major defence establishment in the world.

8.8 Mission Decision — What Would You Do, Cadet?

📖 Mission Log — The Speed Argument

Arjun's unit is evaluating a new autonomous engagement system. The system can identify and engage drone threats in under 200 milliseconds — far faster than any human operator. The argument for full autonomy: by the time a human reacts, the threat drone has already reached the perimeter.

The counter-argument: the system has a 3% misidentification rate. On a busy base with both friendly and hostile drones in the air, a 3% error rate over 1,000 engagements means 30 friendly drones destroyed. And if it misidentifies a manned aircraft, the consequences are catastrophic.

The commander turns to Arjun: "What engagement policy do you recommend?"
