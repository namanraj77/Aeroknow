7.2 The Four Computer Vision Tasks

Computer vision is not one thing — it is a family of related tasks, each answering a different question about what the drone sees. Military UAVs use all four, often simultaneously on the same video feed.

🔍 Classification

What is it?

The entire image is assigned a single label. "This image contains a tank." No location, no box — just a category. Useful for rapid scene categorisation.

Speed: Very fast — simplest task
▭ Object Detection

What is it and where?

Finds every object of interest in the image, draws a bounding box around each one, and assigns a class label with confidence score. Multiple objects, one pass.

The workhorse of military UAV perception — YOLO, SSD, Faster R-CNN
🎨 Semantic Segmentation

What is every pixel?

Every pixel in the image is classified into a category: road, vehicle, building, vegetation, person. No boxes — precise pixel-level understanding of the scene.

Used for terrain analysis, safe landing zone detection, mapping
📺 Object Tracking

Where is it going?

Once an object is detected, tracking maintains a persistent lock on it across frames — even when it moves, rotates, changes size, or is briefly occluded by another object.

Enables persistent surveillance — the drone follows the target automatically
📖 Mission Log — Four Questions at Once

"On the MQ-9, the perception pipeline runs all four simultaneously," the officer explained. "Classification tells command what type of scene we're looking at. Detection finds every vehicle and person in frame. Segmentation maps which areas are road, building, open ground. And tracking locks onto a specific target and follows it even if the drone banks or the target tries to hide. One camera feed, four parallel analyses, all in under fifty milliseconds."

7.3 How Convolutional Neural Networks Actually See

In B06 you learned that neural networks have layers. For image tasks, a specific type of layer called a convolutional layer is what makes image understanding possible. Understanding convolutions conceptually explains why deep learning works so well on visual data.

The Magnifying Glass Analogy

Imagine examining a photograph with a small magnifying glass. You slide it across the image systematically, left to right, top to bottom. At each position, you record what you see: an edge here, a curve there, a dark patch, a bright corner. You build up a map of every local pattern across the image.

That is exactly what a convolutional layer does. The "magnifying glass" is called a filter or kernel — a small grid of learned numbers. It slides across the image, producing a number at each position that indicates how strongly that filter's pattern appeared there. Early layers detect simple things (edges, colours). Deeper layers combine those into complex things (wheels, gun barrels, vehicle silhouettes).

► CNN FEATURE EXTRACTION — What Each Layer Detects Complexity increases with depth
Why "Convolutional" Networks Work So Well on Images

A full-resolution aerial image might be 4000×3000 pixels — 12 million values. A standard neural network would need billions of connections just to process one frame. Convolutional layers use a small filter (e.g. 3×3 pixels) that is applied across the whole image — the same filter, reused at every position. This weight sharing makes CNNs computationally practical and also gives them a property called translation invariance: a wheel is recognised as a wheel whether it appears in the top-left or bottom-right of the image. LeCun et al., 1998

7.4 Object Detection — Finding Everything in One Pass

Object detection is the most operationally important computer vision task for military UAVs. It does two things simultaneously: finds where objects are (bounding box) and what they are (class label with confidence score). Multiple objects, one forward pass, in real time.

The Grid Search Analogy

Imagine dividing a satellite image into a grid of 100 cells. In each cell, the network asks: "Is there an object here? If so, what type and how confident am I?" Then it refines those guesses, merges overlapping boxes, and outputs only the cleanest, most confident detections. This grid-based approach is the core of the YOLO ("You Only Look Once") family — the dominant real-time detector in both commercial and military UAV systems.

Real-Time Performance on Embedded Hardware

YOLOv8 — the current generation standard — achieves 50+ frames per second on a modern GPU and 10-20 FPS on embedded edge processors (NVIDIA Jetson, Hailo-8) used in military drone payloads. At 1080p video, that means a new detection result every 20–50 milliseconds. A vehicle moving at 100 km/h travels 0.6–1.4 metres between detection frames — well within tracking tolerance. Ultralytics, 2023

⚠ The Confidence Score Is Not a Certainty

A detection with 92% confidence means the model has assigned that probability based on learned patterns — it does not mean the model is correct 92% of the time in all conditions. In novel environments (new terrain, unusual lighting, adversarial camouflage), a 92% confident detection can still be wrong. Confidence scores must always be interpreted in the context of the operating environment versus the training data distribution.

7.5 Target Tracking — Keeping Eyes on the Target

Detection finds objects in a single frame. Tracking maintains a persistent lock on a specific object across many frames — even as the drone moves, the camera pans, the target accelerates, or something briefly passes in front of it. This is what makes persistent surveillance possible.

📖 Mission Log — Do Not Lose the Vehicle

"Once you have the target," the operator told Arjun, "you cannot let it disappear into the urban grid. One wrong turn and you lose it in the traffic." She pointed at the screen. The bounding box around the vehicle was locked — moving with it smoothly as it turned, accelerated, slowed. The drone banked to follow. "The tracker handles that. Even if a truck parks between us and the target for two seconds, the tracker predicts where the target should be and re-acquires when line of sight returns."

How Tracking Works

Modern trackers combine detection with prediction. The most widely used approach is Multi-Object Tracking (MOT). Each detected object gets an identity. Between frames, the tracker predicts where each identity will be next (using the Kalman Filter from B05). When the next frame arrives, new detections are matched to existing identities using both position and appearance. If a match is found, the track continues. If not, a new track is started.

Track ID Persistence — Why It Matters Operationally

Tracking assigns each target a persistent ID (e.g. Track 007) that survives across the entire surveillance session. This allows analysts to build a timeline: "Track 007 departed the compound at 14:23, stopped at coordinates X for 11 minutes, then rendezvoused with Track 012 at 15:40." Without persistent track IDs, each detection is an isolated event with no temporal connection. With tracking, the drone produces intelligence — not just observations.
